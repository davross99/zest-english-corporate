Master prepositions and you've taken a giant step towards mastering English.

You already know the moment. An agent or a student who knows the grammar, has passed every test, and still hesitates over "in" or "at," "above" or "over," "in front of" or "near," right in the middle of a sentence. Prepositions are some of the most frequent words in English, and among the last to become automatic, even for fluent speakers. Zest English isn't a bigger curriculum or a longer course. It's a short, focused fix for one specific problem, so the right word stops being a decision and starts being second nature.

This isn't about ability. It's about how the brain learns a language.

If you've sat through a call, a lesson, or a performance review wondering why someone this capable still trips over a small word, you're not looking at a training failure. Words that carry meaning, nouns, verbs, adjectives, tend to stick early. Small connecting words like prepositions don't carry meaning on their own; they only make sense in context, and the brain needs many repetitions before they become automatic. That's true for every learner you have, at every level of fluency, no matter how long they've studied.

Until that repetition happens, there's a brief pause while the brain chooses. On a call, in a meeting, in front of a client, that pause is what you're hearing, and it isn't a lack of ability. It's a completely normal, temporary part of how a second language settles in, and it's the exact gap most training programs never target directly.

Why this matters to your bottom line

If you're weighing this against other places that training budget could go, here's the context. English proficiency worldwide has been declining for four straight years, even as roughly 85% of multinational companies run on English as their working language. Industry commentary on call centre performance consistently ties agent fluency to how efficiently a call gets resolved: a reduction in Average Handling Time signals that agents are actually applying training to solve problems faster, and clearer communication is what lets agents handle more calls and close them out sooner. One BPO outsourcing executive put it plainly: "the real differentiator is English fluency and comprehension" when it comes to customer experience, not accent. If that's the metric you're already being judged on, this is the specific, narrow piece of it Zest English is built to move.

Method

How it works, and why it doesn't need a rollout plan

There's no login system to configure, no course to sit through, no weeks of onboarding before anyone sees value. Zest English works through short, story-based games. Each scene puts the learner inside a real situation and asks them to choose the right preposition to keep the story moving. Every gap has exactly one correct answer, so there's no guessing and no confusion about what counts as right, and no ambiguity for you to adjudicate later.

This works for learners of any age, any level, any background, which matters if your team or your student body isn't a single, tidy cohort. Preposition mistakes don't disappear once someone reaches an advanced level of English, even fluent speakers pause over "in" or "at," "above" or "over." What changes with practice isn't whether someone understands the rule. It's whether the right word comes out without having to think about it.

That's why the method is repetition, not theory. Instead of memorising lists of rules that rarely hold up in real conversation, learners see the same words used correctly again and again inside a story, until the right choice becomes automatic. The whole thing takes minutes, not hours, per session.

Repetition is what turns knowledge into reflex.

Once that repetition happens, the pause disappears. Not because someone tried harder, but because the word simply doesn't need to be searched for anymore. What's left is fluency: the sentence keeps its rhythm, and what comes through is confidence, in a meeting, on a call, in front of a client.

That's the whole pitch. Not a transformation of your entire training programme, not a platform to manage. One specific, well-documented gap in spoken English, closed simply, so it stops costing you calls, confidence, and credibility.
