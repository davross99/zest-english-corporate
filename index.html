It isn't ability. It's how the brain files small words.

Nouns, verbs, and adjectives carry meaning, so they stick early. Prepositions don't carry meaning on their own, they only make sense in context, and context takes repetition to master. That's true of every learner you have, at every level of fluency, however long they've studied.

Give it the repetition it needs, and the hesitation goes away. Not because anyone tried harder. Because the word stops needing to be searched for.

Method

No login to configure. No course to sit through.

Each game drops the learner into a real, story-based scene and asks them to choose the right preposition to keep it moving. Every gap has one right answer in context, so there's no guessing, and nothing left for you to adjudicate later. Answers follow standard usage consistent across British and American English; where the two genuinely diverge, the game is built around the usage they share.

Sessions run minutes, not hours. The same games work for any age, any level, any background, useful when your team, or your student body, isn't one tidy cohort.

Repetition is what turns knowledge into reflex.

Once that repetition happens, the pause disappears. Not because someone tried harder, but because the word simply doesn't need to be searched for anymore. What's left is fluency: the sentence keeps its rhythm, and what comes through is confidence, in a meeting, on a call, in front of a client.

That's the whole pitch. Not a transformation of your entire training programme, not a platform to manage. One specific, well-documented gap in spoken English, closed simply, so it stops costing you calls, confidence, and credibility.
